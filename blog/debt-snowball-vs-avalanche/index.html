Debt Snowball vs. Debt Avalanche: Which Pays Off Debt Faster?

If you've got more than one debt, there are two famous ways to attack them — the snowball and the avalanche. They sound technical, but the idea behind each is simple, and picking the right one for you can be the difference between giving up and getting to zero.

The two methods in one sentence each

  • Debt Snowball: pay minimums on everything, then throw every extra dollar at your smallest balance first. When it's gone, roll that payment onto the next-smallest. Quick wins, big motivation.
  • Debt Avalanche: pay minimums on everything, then throw every extra dollar at your highest interest rate first. Mathematically optimal — you pay the least total interest.

A side-by-side example

Say you have three debts and $200/month extra to throw at them:

Debt Balance Rate
Credit card A $500 24%
Store card $800 19%
Credit card B $3,000 22%
  • Snowball order: Card A ($500) → Store card ($800) → Card B ($3,000). You kill that first $500 debt fast — a real, motivating win in a few weeks.
  • Avalanche order: Card A (24%) → Card B (22%) → Store card (19%). You save the most interest overall.

So which is better?

Here's the honest answer most articles dodge: the best method is the one you'll actually finish.

  • Studies and real-world experience consistently show the snowball wins for most people — not because the math is better (it isn't), but because the early wins keep you going. Motivation is the scarce resource, not arithmetic.
  • If you're highly numbers-driven and won't lose steam, the avalanche saves you the most money.
  • The interest difference between the two is often surprisingly small unless you carry large, high-rate balances.

How to start today

  1. List every debt: name, balance, interest rate, minimum payment.
  2. Pick your method (snowball for momentum, avalanche for math).
  3. Order your debts accordingly.
  4. Pay minimums on all, plus every spare dollar on the #1 target.
  5. When #1 is gone, roll its whole payment onto #2. That "rolling" is where the speed comes from.

Seeing it on paper — balances shrinking, one debt crossed off, then another — is what makes it stick. A debt that just lives in a banking app is easy to ignore. A debt you're physically crossing off a tracker is one you're beating.
