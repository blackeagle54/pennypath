What Is a Sinking Fund? (And How to Start One This Week)

Here's a budgeting secret that changes everything once it clicks: most "unexpected" expenses are completely expected. Your car registration, the holidays, your friend's wedding, the vet visit — you know these are coming. You just don't know the exact date. A sinking fund is how you stop them from blowing up your month.

The definition

A sinking fund is money you set aside a little at a time for a specific, known future expense.

Instead of getting hit with a $1,200 holiday-spending bill in December and reaching for a credit card, you set aside $100/month starting in January. By December, the money is just… there. No stress, no debt.

Sinking fund vs. emergency fund

People mix these up:

  • Emergency fund = for the truly unknown (job loss, a surprise medical bill). One big pot, usually 3–6 months of expenses.
  • Sinking fund = for the known-but-irregular (car maintenance, gifts, annual subscriptions, travel). Many small, named pots.

You want both. The sinking funds protect the emergency fund from being raided for things that were never actually emergencies.

Common sinking funds to start with

  • 🎄 Holidays & gifts
  • 🚗 Car maintenance & registration
  • 🏥 Medical / dental
  • ✈️ Travel & vacations
  • 🎂 Birthdays
  • 🏠 Home repairs
  • 🐕 Pet care
  • 💻 Annual subscriptions (renewals you pay once a year)

The only math you need

Monthly contribution = Total goal ÷ Months until you need it

Holiday budget of $1,200, and it's January? $1,200 ÷ 12 = $100/month. Car registration of $300 due in 6 months? $300 ÷ 6 = $50/month. Round up if you can.

How to start this week

  1. Brainstorm every irregular expense you can think of over the next 12 months.
  2. For each, write the total cost and when it's due.
  3. Divide to find the monthly amount.
  4. Open one savings account (or use named "pots"/envelopes) and automate the transfers.
  5. Track it — watching the balance climb is what keeps you doing it.

That last step matters more than it sounds. A simple visual tracker — even just coloring in a thermometer as a fund fills up — turns a chore into something a little satisfying.
