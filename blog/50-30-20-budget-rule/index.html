The 50/30/20 Budget Rule: The Simplest Way to Budget Your Money

If you've ever opened a budgeting app, felt overwhelmed by 40 spending categories, and quietly closed it again — the 50/30/20 rule is for you. It's the budget for people who don't want to think about budgeting very much. Here's exactly how it works.

What is the 50/30/20 rule?

You split your after-tax income into three buckets:

  • 50% — Needs. The things you genuinely have to pay: rent or mortgage, groceries, utilities, insurance, minimum debt payments, transportation to work.
  • 30% — Wants. The fun, optional stuff: dining out, streaming, hobbies, travel, the nice coffee.
  • 20% — Savings & debt payoff. Your emergency fund, retirement, investments, and any extra debt payments beyond the minimums.

That's the whole system. Three numbers. No spreadsheet PhD required.

A quick example

Say you take home $3,000/month:

Bucket % Amount
Needs 50% $1,500
Wants 30% $900
Savings/Debt 20% $600

Now you have a target for each area. If your "needs" are creeping past $1,500, that's a signal — maybe rent is too high a share of your income, or a "want" snuck into the needs column (looking at you, three streaming services).

Why it works

  1. It's memorable. You can do the math in your head at the grocery store.
  2. It builds savings automatically. That 20% is non-negotiable, so saving stops being an afterthought.
  3. It's flexible. The percentages are a starting point, not a law. High cost-of-living area? You might run 60/20/20 for a while and adjust as your income grows.

How to actually start (this week)

  1. Find your monthly take-home pay (after taxes).
  2. Multiply by 0.50, 0.30, and 0.20 to get your three targets.
  3. List your current expenses and sort each into Needs / Wants / Savings.
  4. Compare reality to your targets. Adjust one thing this month — just one.

The hardest part isn't the math; it's seeing it on paper. Something about writing your income at the top of a page and dividing it up makes the plan feel real in a way a banking app never does.
